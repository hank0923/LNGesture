    [r 24.4] 24.4 Judicial officer or court officer as examiner

    (cf SCR Part 27, rule 1C; DCR Part 25, rule 3)

    (1)

    A judicial officer or other officer of the court may not act as an examiner otherwise than with the concurrence of the senior judicial officer.

    (2)

    An applicant for an order for a person’s examination, outside New South Wales, by a judicial officer or other officer of the court may request the proposed examiner to certify the amount which in his or her opinion should be paid into court as provision for expenses of the examination.

    (3)

    Such an order may be expressed to be conditional on the payment into court, by such person and within such time as the court may specify, of not less than the amount certified in accordance with subrule (2).

    (4)

    If satisfied that the amount paid or payable into court is or may be insufficient to provide for the expenses of the examination, the court, on application of the registrar:
    • (a)may make an order directing the party on whose application the order for examination was made to pay into court such further amount, and within such time, as the court may specify, and
    • (b)may make an order:
      • (i)staying the proceedings, so far as they concern the whole or any part of any claim for relief by that party, or
      • (ii)suspending the operation of the order for examination,

      until such payment is made.

    (5)

    The registrar must apply so much of the amount paid into court as may from time to time be required for the purpose in the payment, to or at the direction of the examiner, of:
    • (a)expenses incurred in relation to the examination, or
    • (b)advances for expenses to be incurred in relation to the examination.

    (6)

    Any amount paid under subrule (5) that is not required for expenses in relation to the examination must be repaid into court.

    (7)

    After the conclusion of the examination, on the examiner certifying that no expenses in relation to the examination remain unpaid or unrecouped from the money in court, the registrar must, subject to any order of the court, refund to the person by whom the money was paid into court (and, if more than one, in the same proportions as their respective payments into court) any money remaining in court.


    NOTES
    [24.4.5] Examiner — persons who may be appointed — generally
    Legislation cited in this paragraph
    • (NSW) Supreme Court Act 1970 s 83.
    • (NSW) Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 2005 r 24.4, r 24.9.

    Any person may be appointed an examiner and may administer any oath required for the taking of the evidence: Supreme Court Act 1970 s 83. The choice of the appropriate person in any particular case will depend upon a number of considerations including the convenience of the witness and the parties, the nature of the evidence, its materiality to the proceedings and the nature of any controversy that may exist in relation to it. The appointment of a practising lawyer as the examiner will usually have the twin advantages of combining flexibility as to the time and place of the examination, with knowledge of the appropriate procedures required to be followed: see r 24.9. (Knowledge of the procedural requirements is especially important where the examination is being conducted for the purpose of foreign proceedings — because of the statutory restrictions on the scope of the examination: see [24.9.5] and [SCR 58.2.1]). Where issues of credibility arise, or where the evidence is particularly material to the resolution of the proceedings, it may be appropriate to appoint a judge as the examiner: see r 24.4.

    [24.4.10] Examiner — persons who may be appointed — foreign examination venue

    The examinations conducted in another jurisdiction must comply with the laws of that jurisdiction. Those laws may require the examination to be conducted before consular officers, may limit the persons who may be examined and may not provide any powers to compel attendance or production of documents. Subject to any such limitation, the choice of examiner is a matter for the appointing court and depends upon the materiality of the evidence and the issues involved. It has been common for the examination to be carried out either by a forum lawyer or official. But a lawyer or other responsible person in the examination jurisdiction can also be appointed as the examiner.

    [24.4.15] Judge as examiner
    Legislation cited in this paragraph
    • (NSW) Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 2005 r 24.11.
    • (NSW) Supreme Court Act 1970 s 39.

    The limited role permitted to an examiner by the provisions of r 24.11 will usually mean that it is inappropriate for a judge to be made an examiner: Henderson v Primmer (1986) 68 ACTR 9; 84 FLR 265  citing Practice Note: Chancery Deposition [1981] 1 WLR 1560 at 1561 . However, this rule recognises that there may be occasions where the circumstances require that a judge be appointed. The rule makes any such possible appointment subject to the prior concurrence of the Chief Justice who, in accordance with s 39 of the Supreme Court Act 1970, has a principal role in the intra curial arrangement of the business of the court. The procedure to be followed when making such an application is for a notice of motion and affidavit to be filed in the usual way. (Applications in the Supreme Court should comply with Practice Note SC Gen 9.) The application will then be referred to the Chief Judge of the relevant division of the court and the Chief Judge will consult with the Chief Justice. A form of order that was made on one such appointment, albeit one made before the introduction of the present rule, is set out in Universal Seaways Pty Ltd v Derby and Co (Aust) Pty Ltd (NSWSC, Rogers J, CL 12078/78, 17 April 1980, unreported) (see the “Practice Decisions” guide card in vol 3 at [456,210]). The order made in that case would now require some amendment to comply with the present rule.

    [24.4.20] Payment of examination expenses
    Legislation cited in this paragraph
    • (NSW) Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 2005 r 24.4(2).

    Despite the requirement in r 24.4(2) it has been said that it is desirable for the amount of the contemplated expenses to be determined by a judicial officer other than the judge who is to be the examiner: Australian Securities and Investments Commission v Rich (2004) 49 ACSR 578; [2004] NSWSC 467; BC200403134 at [80] ; and, for the judge to formally certify the determination actually made by that other judge: see Australian Securities and Investments Commission v Rich (2004) 50 ACSR 357; [2004] NSWSC 750; BC200405390 .
